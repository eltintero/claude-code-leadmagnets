## Step 1: Open the Claude Desktop App

If you haven't installed it yet, download the Claude app from Anthropic's website. Install it like any other app — drag it to Applications on Mac, or run the installer on Windows.

Open the app. Sign in with your Claude account (the same one you use on claude.ai).

You'll see a familiar chat interface. This is normal Claude — the same experience you've used before. But we're about to unlock the part that makes it different.

---

## Step 2: Give Claude Access to a Folder

This is the key step. This is where Claude Code becomes Claude Code.

In the app, look for the option to add a folder or give Claude file access. The exact location may vary depending on your app version, but it's typically in Settings or in the conversation window itself — you'll see an option to "Add folder" or "Connect files."

Pick a folder on your computer that contains real business documents. Good options:

- Your proposals folder
- Your client communication folder
- A project folder with SOPs or process documents
- Your marketing materials folder

Don't pick something sensitive yet (we'll cover data policies later). Pick something you work with regularly — something where you'd love an assistant who already knows the content.

Once you grant access, Claude can now read every file in that folder. Not upload it to the internet — read it locally, on your machine, in real time.

This is the moment it stops being a chatbot and starts being an assistant.

---

## Step 3: Your First Real Prompt

Now let's do something useful. Here are three prompts you can try right now, depending on what folder you connected:

### If you connected your proposals folder:

> Read through my recent proposals. What's the typical structure I follow? What do I usually include in the pricing section? Summarize the patterns you see — I want to make sure the next proposal follows the same format.

Claude will read your actual proposals — not guess what a proposal should look like. It'll tell you *your* patterns, *your* structure, *your* pricing format. You'll probably learn something about your own process you hadn't noticed.

### If you connected your client communications:

> Look at how I communicate with clients across these emails and documents. Describe my communication style — tone, formality level, how I open and close messages, what phrases I tend to use. I want you to match this style in future work.

This is how Claude starts learning your voice. Not from a generic template — from your actual writing. The output becomes a mirror that shows you how you communicate, which you can then refine and save for future sessions.

### If you connected your marketing or content folder:

> Read all the content in this folder. What are the main topics I cover? What's the tone and voice? What kind of examples or data points do I tend to use? Create a one-page brand voice summary based on what you see.

Claude just built you a brand voice guide from your own work. Most companies pay thousands for this. You got a working first draft in 30 seconds.

## What Just Happened

Take a second to notice what was different about that interaction compared to regular Claude chat.

**You didn't paste anything.** No copying from documents, no "here's some context," no walls of text in the chat window. Claude went and read the files itself.

**You didn't explain your business.** No "we're a marketing agency that..." or "our typical client is..." Claude figured that out from your actual documents.

**The output was specific to you.** Not generic advice. Not "here's what a typical proposal looks like." Your structure. Your voice. Your patterns.

That's the shift. Claude Code doesn't need you to be the middleman between your business and the AI. It goes straight to the source.

## Step 4: Make It Do Something

Analysis is impressive. But the real value is in output — things you can use.

Try one of these:

### Draft something new based on what it learned:

> Based on the proposal structure you just analyzed, draft a new proposal for a client named [Name] who needs [brief description of what they need]. Use our standard format, our typical pricing structure, and match the tone of our previous proposals.

### Create a template from your existing work:

> Based on the client emails you analyzed, create a reusable template for follow-up emails after a first meeting. Include placeholders for the client name, what we discussed, and next steps. Match my exact communication style.

### Summarize and organize:

> Read all the documents in this folder and create a summary document — organized by topic — of everything we've written about [subject]. Include the key points, any data or numbers mentioned, and flag anything that seems outdated or contradictory.

Notice the pattern: you're not asking Claude to imagine what your business does. You're asking it to work *with* what's already there. Read this, analyze that, create something new based on what you found.

That's delegation. And it only works when Claude has access to your files.

## The Permission Model — Why Claude Keeps Asking

You'll notice that Claude asks for permission before doing certain things. Reading a new folder, creating a file, modifying an existing document — it checks with you first.

This isn't a bug. It's a feature you'll grow to appreciate.

Claude Code operates on a principle of controlled access. You decide what it can see. You decide what it can change. You approve actions before they happen. Nothing happens behind your back.

For your first few sessions, you'll see these permission prompts frequently. Over time, you can adjust the settings to give Claude more autonomy in areas where you trust it — and keep tighter controls where it matters.

Think of it like giving keys to a new employee. You start with supervised access. As trust builds, you hand over more.

For now, just say yes when it asks to read the folder you've already chosen. We'll cover the full permission system in Module 5.

## Conversations vs. Projects: The Key Difference

In regular Claude (the chat), every conversation starts fresh. You ask a question, get an answer, and that's it. The next conversation has no memory of the last one.

In Claude Code, you're working inside a **project context**. As long as you're in the same folder, Claude remembers:

- The files it's read
- The instructions you've given
- The patterns it's identified
- The work it's produced

This means your second conversation in the same folder is better than the first. And the tenth is dramatically better than the second. Claude builds up an understanding of your work over time — not just from the files, but from your corrections, preferences, and feedback.

"That proposal was too formal — we're more casual with this client."

"Good email, but we always include pricing in the first follow-up, not the second."

"Don't use bullet points in client-facing documents. We write in paragraphs."

Every correction makes the next output better. Claude doesn't just follow instructions — it learns your preferences within the session and carries them forward.

## Quick Wins You Can Get Today

Before moving to Module 3, here are five things you can do right now that will save you real time this week:

1. **Audit your own writing.** Point Claude at your sent emails or published content and ask: "What's my communication style? What are my strongest patterns? What's inconsistent?" You'll get a mirror you've never had.

2. **Build a template from your best work.** Find the proposal, email, or document you're most proud of. Ask Claude to reverse-engineer it into a reusable template with placeholders. Now you've got a starting point for every future version.

3. **Summarize a messy folder.** That Google Drive folder with 47 files from the last project? Point Claude at it and say "What's in here? Organize it by topic and tell me what's important." Instant clarity.

4. **Draft a process document.** Ask Claude to read how you communicate with clients across multiple documents and produce a "How We Work With Clients" one-pager. Share it with your team. You just created documentation that probably didn't exist.

5. **Write next week's content.** Point Claude at your previous social posts or newsletter issues and say "Write three LinkedIn posts in the same voice and style, about [topic]." You'll have drafts in seconds that actually sound like you.

None of these require any technical setup beyond what you just did — open the app, connect a folder, talk to Claude.
