Same Question, 10x Better Answer

Most people type a sentence into Claude and hope for the best. Then they complain the output is "meh."

The problem isn't Claude. It's the input.

Here's the formula that changes everything:
Context + Format + Examples = Great Output

Context = who you are, what you're working on, and why. Your Claude Project handles the organizational context automatically. Your prompt adds the situational context: "I'm writing a follow-up email to a prospect who attended our webinar yesterday. They asked about pricing but seemed hesitant."

Format = what the output should look like. "Write a 3-paragraph email." "Give me a bullet-point summary." "Create a table comparing these options." "Keep it under 200 words." Claude follows formatting instructions precisely when you give them.

Examples = show, don't just tell. "Here's a follow-up email I sent last week that worked well: [paste it]. Write something similar for this new prospect." One example is worth 100 words of instruction.

Bonus: Extended Thinking. For complex problems — strategy, analysis, multi-step reasoning — toggle on Extended Thinking. Claude will spend more time reasoning before responding. It's the difference between a quick reaction and a considered answer.

Today's Exercise • 20 minutes

Step 1: Pick a real work task you need to do today. An email, a proposal section, a client response — something real.

Step 2: First, try the lazy prompt. Just type what you need in one sentence. Save the output.

Step 3: Now, apply the formula. New conversation, same Project:
[CONTEXT] I'm [your role]. I need to [specific task]. The situation is [relevant details]. The audience is [who will read this].

[FORMAT] Write this as [format]. Keep it [length]. Tone should be [tone reference from your brand voice guide].

[EXAMPLE] Here's an example of something similar I've done before that worked well:
[Paste a real example]

Now write [the thing you need].

Step 4: Compare the two outputs side by side. The difference should be dramatic.

Step 5: Try the same exercise outside your Project (in a regular Claude conversation). Then try it inside your Project. Notice how the Project context fills in gaps you didn't even have to mention.
