# Module 3: The CLAUDE.md File — Teaching Claude Who Your Business Is

---

## The Moment It Clicks

Here's what happens the first time most business owners try Claude Code on real work.

They open the app. They point it at a folder — maybe their company docs, their proposals, their marketing materials. They ask it to draft something: a client email, a proposal, a social media post.

Claude delivers something that looks... fine. Grammatically correct. Professional. But wrong.

Wrong tone. Wrong offer structure. A proposal that doesn't match how your team prices projects. A client email that sounds like it was written by a Fortune 500 marketing department — not a 15-person agency in Miami.

The tool works. But it doesn't know your business.

That's the gap a CLAUDE.md file fills.

---

## What Is CLAUDE.md?

CLAUDE.md is a simple text file you create and place in a folder on your computer. Every time Claude Code works inside that folder, it reads this file first — before it reads anything else, before it produces any output.

Think of it as a cheat sheet for a new hire. The way you'd brief someone on their first day: "Here's who we are. Here's how we talk to clients. Here's what we never do. Here's what matters."

Except this hire has perfect memory, never forgets your instructions, and follows them every single time.

No CLAUDE.md? Claude treats your business like any business. Generic language, generic assumptions, generic output.

A good CLAUDE.md? Claude works like someone who's been at your company for a year.

---

## Your First CLAUDE.md (The 15-Minute Version)

You don't need a perfect file to start. You need a real one.

Open any text editor — even Notes or TextEdit works. Create a new file. Name it `CLAUDE.md`. Save it in the folder where you keep your work documents. And write something like this:

```
# About Our Business

## Who We Are
[One paragraph. What does your company do? Who are your customers?
What makes you different from competitors?]

## How We Talk
- Our tone is [friendly / professional / casual / formal]
- We always [use the client's first name / lead with data / etc.]
- We never [use jargon / make promises without timelines / etc.]
- Our brand voice sounds like [a trusted advisor / a sharp friend / etc.]

## Our Services & Pricing
- [Service 1]: [brief description, price range]
- [Service 2]: [brief description, price range]
- [How we typically structure proposals]

## Our Team
- [Name]: [Role — what they handle]
- [Name]: [Role — what they handle]

## Important Rules
- [Things Claude should NEVER say or do for this business]
- [Things that are always true — "we always follow up within 24 hours"]
- [Client-facing language we avoid]

## Our Clients
- Typical client: [industry, size, common pain points]
- Notable clients: [names if you want Claude to reference them]
```

That's it. Save it. Open Claude Code in that folder. Ask it to write a client email, draft a proposal, or create a social post.

The difference is immediate. Not perfect — but the gap between "generic AI output" and "output that sounds like us" shrinks from the first conversation.

## Going Deeper: What a Real CLAUDE.md Looks Like

The 15-minute version gets you started. But a CLAUDE.md that actually performs across your business — not just one email, but every proposal, every Slack message to clients, every internal document — needs more depth.

Here's what a mature CLAUDE.md covers:

### Company Context
Not just what you do — *how* you think about what you do. Your mission, your positioning, how you're different from competitors. When Claude understands your positioning, it stops writing generic copy and starts writing copy that reinforces your brand with every sentence.

### Brand Voice & Communication Style
How do you talk to clients vs. prospects vs. your team? What words do you use? What words do you avoid? Do you lead with data or stories? Formal or casual? What does an email from your company *sound* like?

This is the section most people underestimate. Your brand voice is the difference between "Claude writes decent emails" and "Claude writes emails I can send without editing."

### Services, Pricing & Sales Process
What do you sell? How is it structured? What are the typical packages or tiers? How do you handle objections? What's the discovery process? When Claude knows your sales playbook, it can draft proposals, follow-ups, and objection responses that match how your team actually sells.

### Team Structure & Roles
Who does what? Who handles client communication? Who approves proposals? Who manages projects? This matters when Claude drafts anything that involves your team — intros, handoffs, project updates.

### Client & Industry Knowledge
Who are your typical clients? What industries? What pain points do they have? What language do they use to describe their problems? When Claude knows your clients, it writes to them — not at them.

### Processes & SOPs
How do you onboard a new client? What's the workflow for a project kickoff? How do you handle a support request? These are the processes that Claude can help draft, document, and follow — but only if it knows they exist.

### What Not to Do
This section is often the most valuable. "Never promise delivery dates without checking with the PM." "Never mention competitor names." "Never discount below 15% without approval." "Never use the word 'cheap' — we say 'accessible.'"

The "don'ts" prevent the kind of mistakes that damage client relationships. And they're the things you only discover Claude gets wrong *after* it gets them wrong.

## The Part Nobody Talks About

Writing a CLAUDE.md forces you to put on paper things your company has never written down.

How *do* you actually talk to clients? "We're friendly and professional" — but what does that mean in practice? Do you use first names or last names? Do you open with small talk or get to the point? Do you sign off with "Best" or "Cheers" or just your name?

What's your real pricing logic? Not the rate card — the actual decision process. When do you offer a discount? When do you push back? When do you walk away?

How do you handle a client who's unhappy? There's a way your best account manager does it — and then there's what everyone else wings.

Most companies don't have clear answers to these questions. The knowledge lives in the heads of the people who've been there since the beginning. It gets passed down through Slack threads, overheard conversations, and trial and error.

A CLAUDE.md forces all of that into writing. And that's valuable far beyond AI — it's the kind of documentation that makes onboarding new employees faster, keeps your team consistent, and prevents the "that's not how we do it here" conversations.

But extracting that knowledge, organizing it, and keeping it current? That's where it stops being a 15-minute exercise.

## What Changes When You Scale

One person with one CLAUDE.md for one type of work? Manageable. You write it, update it when things change, and you're good.

Now picture this at company scale:

- **Multiple departments** each need different context. Sales needs the pitch playbook. Operations needs the SOPs. Marketing needs the brand voice. Customer support needs the escalation rules. One CLAUDE.md doesn't cover all of it.

- **10 team members** all using Claude. Are they getting consistent output? Is Claude giving your sales team the same pricing info it's giving your account managers? Who keeps all the files aligned?

- **Connected tools** like Slack, Google Drive, your CRM, your calendar. Each integration needs its own set of instructions — what to read, what format to follow, what's off limits.

- **Client-specific rules** that vary by account. One client requires formal communication. Another prefers Slack over email. A third has compliance requirements that affect what you can share with AI tools.

- **Sensitive information** that must be handled carefully. Client data, financial details, contracts, HR information. Which folders should Claude have access to? Which should it never touch? Who decides?

- **Keeping it current.** Your services change. Your team changes. Your pricing changes. Your processes evolve. Every time something shifts and the CLAUDE.md doesn't get updated, Claude starts giving outdated answers — and your team starts losing trust in the tool.

The file itself is simple. A system of CLAUDE.md files across an organization — consistent, current, department-specific, aligned with client rules and data policies — that's where most companies stall.
